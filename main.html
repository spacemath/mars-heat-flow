Heat-flow and Temperature Differences in the Martian Crust

NASA/JPL-Caltech

The Idea

Stop Animation
To keep heat inside your home you add insulation to the walls. From experience, we know that if you plot the temperature through a wall it will be a straight line. We also know that the slope of that line depends on the quality of the insulation---the [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conductivity heat conductivity]. For the same amount of escaping heat, a high quality insulation will create a higher slope (bigger temperature difference between outside and inside). The core of Mars is hot (like your home).
By measuring the slope of the temperature change in the crust (which insulates like your walls) we can estimate the amount of heat ([https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_flux heat flux]) escaping the surface. As part of NASA's [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/InSight insight] mission, the HP3 instrument (nickname: The Mole, shown below) will drill 5m into the surface and measure the temperature every 30cm. In this lab, you will plot the temperature at various depths in the Martian soil. From the slope, and heat conductivity of the crust, you can compute the heat flux of Mars.

The Experiment

Our ideas about heat-flux can be written simply: $$ T(x) = a + bx$$ That is, the temperature $T$ (deg. K) is just a line (depending on the depth, $x$, below the surface) with slope $b$ and intercept $a$ (but, we don't care about $a$ here). From theory, we know the slope is $b=F/k$, where $F$ (W/m2) is the heat flux and $k$ (W/mK) represents the quality of the "insulation". The Model

The data you entered above is plotted on the right. Drag the green circles to model the data with a line, and note the parameters (slope $a$ and intercept $b$) at the top. Recall that $b=F/k$, and since we have an estimate for $b$ it remains to figure out a value for the thermal conductivity $k$. The value of $k$ varies over the surface:
  • Thin crust: $k=0.1$ (W/mK)
  • North pole: $k=0.08$ (W/mK)
  • [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tharsis Tharsis]: $k=0.05$ (W/mK)
  • Thick crust: $k=0.02$ (W/mK)
